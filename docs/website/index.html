How It Works

Three simple concepts. No jargon.

🏊

Posting Takes Effort

(Swimming)

When you post, your device does a bit of computational work. About 30 seconds of waiting. Every post is a stroke - effort that moves conversation forward.

Why?

  • Stops spam (bots can't post thousands of times)
  • Makes you think before posting
  • Makes advertising economically stupid

Think of it as a cover charge - low enough that anyone serious can get in, but high enough to make spam pointless.

🌊

Content Fades

(Drifting)

Posts don't last forever. Without engagement, they drift away.

This means:

  • No one moderates what's "appropriate" - time does
  • Embarrassing old posts fade naturally
  • Storage stays small (your phone can hold everything)
  • Popular content survives
  • Ignored content disappears
  • Your embarrassing hot take from 3 AM? Gone in a week if no one cared.

Smart Decay:

Decay adjusts automatically. Busy spaces decay faster to stay manageable. Quiet spaces let content live longer. Half-life ranges from 1 to 30 days based on activity.

Think of it like footprints in sand. The good ones get preserved. The rest wash away.

🔀

You Can Leave

(Forking)

If a community gets captured or goes wrong, you can fork away.

This means:

  • Take your community with you
  • No one can trap you

Like if any subreddit could become its own independent site.

Why Swimchain Can't Have Ads

(not "won't" - literally can't)

What advertisers need:

❌ Guaranteed visibility → No feed to inject into
❌ Targeting by demographics → No data collection
❌ Metrics to prove ROI → No tracking
❌ Someone to pay → No company
❌ Way to skip the line → Everyone waits 30s
Advertiser: "I want to promote my product!"
Swimchain: "Sure. You'll wait 30 seconds like everyone else, hope someone navigates to your space, get no metrics proving anyone saw it, and watch it fade if nobody cares."

That's not advertising. That's just posting.

The protocol makes ads pointless. We didn't ban them. There's just no way to make them work.

"But what if they just spam anyway?"

Let's walk through a determined spammer's attack:

1
Spammer floods a space with promotional content

Cost: ~30 seconds of CPU per post. They have resources. They flood anyway.

2
They farm engagement to keep content alive

Content decays without engagement, so they create fake accounts and engage with themselves. Cost: 60 seconds of CPU per month per post to keep it from fading.

3
Now what?

They've spent compute keeping spam alive. But here's where the protocol breaks their plan:

Defense Layer 1: You don't sync what you don't view

Content blobs only download when you choose to view them. The spammer's promotional images? They're sitting on their storage, not yours. Unless you navigate to that content and view it, you never host it, never download it, never even know it exists.

Their spam, their bandwidth. Not yours.

Defense Layer 2: Spaces fracture under load

As a space grows, it splits into branches based on content hashes. You only sync the branches containing conversations you care about. The spammer's content ends up in a branch nobody navigates to.

They're shouting into a room that got subdivided. And you're not in their subdivision.

Defense Layer 3: Nobody has to host their stuff

Remember: you only serve content you've viewed. If nobody looks at spam, nobody hosts spam. The spammer has to keep their own computers running 24/7 to serve their own content. The moment they turn off, their content becomes unavailable.

The spammer's position:
  • Paid CPU for every post
  • Paying ongoing CPU to keep it alive
  • Hosting it themselves (nobody else will)
  • In a branch nobody syncs
  • With zero metrics proving anyone saw anything

At some point, even determined spammers do the math.

Why Sponsorship?

Every identity needs someone to vouch for it. This creates accountability chains:

  • Sybil Resistance: Can't create thousands of bots - each needs a real sponsor
  • Accountability: If your sponsee spams, your reputation takes a hit too
  • Natural Growth: Networks grow through real social connections, not bot farms
  • No KYC: Pseudonymous but accountable - the sponsor knows you're real

The Consequence Chain

If someone you sponsor misbehaves:

  • Their sponsor (you) gets 100% of the consequence
  • Your sponsor gets 50% of the consequence
  • Beyond 2 hops: negligible

This makes sponsors careful. No one vouches for strangers lightly.

Don't Know Anyone?

Residents and above can post public sponsorship offers:

  • Browse available offers in community directories
  • Some require higher PoW, some require applications
  • All require the sponsor to approve you (no automated claiming)
  • Probationary sponsorships let sponsors take chances with reduced risk

It's not instant. It's not easy. That's intentional - friction filters for commitment.

The Bootstrapping Problem

100 genesis identities were created at launch. They're the roots of the sponsorship forest - no special powers, just the starting points. Everyone else grows from there.

Wait, But...

Who runs the servers?

Nobody. There are no servers.

Your app connects directly to other people's apps. Like BitTorrent for conversation.

If every developer quit tomorrow, the network keeps running - because it's just people's phones talking to each other.

Isn't this just crypto stuff?

No. No tokens, no coins, nothing to buy or trade. We use some blockchain-like tech for timestamps, but this is a place to talk, not an investment vehicle.

What if I don't know anyone to sponsor me?

Residents and higher can post public sponsorship offers for newcomers:

  • Browse community directories for open offers
  • Some require higher PoW commitment, some just an application
  • Probationary sponsorships let sponsors take chances on unknowns

Yes, it takes effort. That's the point. The friction filters for people who actually want to participate, not drive-by accounts.

What about illegal content/harassment?

Illegal content (CSAM, terrorism) is blocked at the protocol level using industry-standard hash databases. Nodes refuse to store or serve matching hashes.

Spam and harassment uses community attestation:

  • 3 Residents from different sponsor trees can flag content as spam
  • Flagged content decays 6x faster (4-hour half-life instead of days)
  • 5 Lifeguards can counter-attest to cancel false positives
  • Attesters are visible and accountable - "the judges are judged"

The honest truth: This is acceleration, not removal. Spam fades fast but doesn't vanish instantly. False positives can be rescued. It's not perfect - but it's transparent and auditable, unlike platform moderation decisions made in a black box.

Why would I want posts to disappear?

Because permanence is a bug, not a feature.

  • That tweet you regret? Gone.
  • That argument from 2018? Gone.
  • Your embarrassing hot take? Gone.

Content persists when people care about it. If no one's engaging, why keep it around?

This sounds hard to use.

If you've ever used a forum, you can use this. The main difference is waiting 30 seconds when you post. That's it. The apps handle everything else - syncing, storage, connecting to peers. You just navigate to spaces and talk to people.

Honestly, the hardest part is unlearning the habits from algorithmic feeds. There's no infinite scroll. You actually have to decide what you want to look at. Some people find that refreshing. Others find it annoying. We can't tell you which you'll be.

Which interface should I use?

  • New to this? Start with the Forum client - it's the most complete.
  • Want it fast? Microblog is lightweight and quick.
  • Got a community? Chat gives real-time vibes.
  • Just curious? Search lets you browse without committing.
  • Developer? CLI + API - you know what to do.
